Ventures

Co-Founder at Optivia

Optivia is an enterprise AI engineering orchestration platform: it turns complex software initiatives into optimized human-agent execution graphs, then builds specialized AI agent fleets to execute, verify, and accelerate the work. The execution graph is the product; the agent fleet is the AI execution layer inside it.

  • Optivia takes a large software initiative (the goal, the codebase, tickets, docs, prior decisions) and breaks it into a graph of work nodes, where every node knows what it depends on, what context it needs, and what success looks like.
  • I designed the scoring engine that rates each node on complexity, risk, dependency load, context requirement, verification need, and AI suitability, then routes it to a human engineer, an AI agent fleet, a reviewer, or an approval gate.
  • For the nodes AI can handle, I built the fleet generator: it composes a team of specialized agents (architect, implementer, verifier, docs) with role-specific context and tools, and puts verification between roles so one agent's mistake doesn't spread.
  • I also designed the scheduling and the economics: critical-path ordering so work never blocks, and cost-aware model routing that picks the cheapest model capable of each task.
  • The architecture is patent pending and I'm lead author on the research paper. The MVP ships this same system scaled down for solo builders; the long-term product is enterprise.

Agents live as cards on an infinite spatial canvas, every tool call from every agent funnels into one approval queue, each agent works in its own isolated git worktree, and all of it runs entirely on your own machine.

Neurotech & ML Researcher at ThinkNeuro LLC

A skin-adhered patch that detects an opioid overdose as it happens and gets help moving before the person can't. The clinical key: overdoses trace a distinctive biphasic signature, an autonomic panic spike, then a rapid collapse into respiratory depression, that is measurably different from anything else, including exercise.

  • The device is a skin-adhered patch that detects opioid overdoses as they happen. An Arduino Nano ESP32 reads a GSR sensor, a MAX30102 pulse oximeter, and an accelerometer, runs the classifier on-device, and sends alerts over Wi-Fi and BLE.
  • The detection target is the biphasic overdose signature: skin conductance spikes to 14–25 µS in the panic phase, then collapses below 1.5 µS as the person loses consciousness. Movement data separates this from exercise, which otherwise looks similar.
  • I built the dataset, because no real one exists: a generator producing 150 synthetic subjects and ~200 hours of sensor data, with every physiological range pulled from published clinical research.
  • I trained and compared three models: logistic regression to verify which features actually matter, and GRU/LSTM networks for deployment. The GRU ships, quantized to INT8 so it runs on the patch itself.
  • I ran the FMEA risk analysis across the device (top risk: overfitting to clean synthetic data; second: motion artifacts on the pulse oximeter), built the React Native app that alerts caregivers and first responders, and I'm co-authoring the academic paper. Patent in progress.

ML Researcher at UC Riverside Lab

Where will traffic break down next? Road networks are graphs, so the question becomes a GNN problem at metropolitan scale: heterogeneous, real-time, geospatial data over networks too large to fit a model naively.

  • The project predicts where traffic congestion will form across a metropolitan road network, using graph neural networks on live geospatial data. I assist the Ph.D. researcher leading it.

ClearAuth

View source↗

Prior authorization is the paperwork that delays care for millions. ClearAuth compresses it to one paste: a clinician drops in a clinical note, and five agents take it from raw text to a filed, audited packet on the payer's portal.

  • Prior authorization is the insurance paperwork a doctor files before treatment. ClearAuth automates it end to end: paste a clinical note, and five agents take it to a filed packet on the payer's portal.
  • The pipeline: extraction structures the note into ICD-10 and CPT codes, criteria checks the payer's coverage rules, formfill assembles the packet, compliance audits it, and a browser agent submits it once the physician approves.
  • All five agents sit behind one model factory (Claude or Gemini) with deterministic fallbacks, so the whole flow runs even with zero API keys.
  • Every step streams live to the CRM over SSE and appends a SHA-256 audit entry; the service is instrumented with OpenTelemetry, and payer responses come back through webhooks.

Osteon

View source↗

An AI agent that autonomously designs orthopedic implants by generating parametric geometry in Blender and iterating it against biomechanical stress analysis, engineered so the design loop survives any model, provider, or tool failing mid-run.

  • An AI agent that designs orthopedic implants: it generates parametric implant geometry in Blender, places it against the bone, runs finite-element stress analysis, and iterates until a candidate survives the load profile.
  • Three MCP servers run the loop: Localize places the implant against the femur geometry, Synthesize generates the parametric mesh, and Evaluate runs the FEA engine and renders stress heatmaps.
  • Built for a resilience-themed hackathon, so failure is the design constraint: model calls route through a gateway to AWS Bedrock with a 3-rung fallback ladder, and every stage returns a schema-valid result even when a model or tool dies mid-run.
  • Blender runs headless through Python scripting, and a Flask webapp serves renders of the implant sitting inside the femur.

HealthFlow

View source↗

A paramedic talks; the system does the rest. Nine agents take live voice dictation from the field through structuring, diagnosis, and safety screening to physician-approved orders committed in the EHR, in under a minute. It took 1st place.

  • Won 1st place at the Scalekit × Apify Hackathon. A paramedic dictates in the field; nine agents turn the voice transcript into structured FHIR R4 records, run differential diagnosis with confidence scores, and screen for drug interactions, catching Warfarin + tPA at 100% in evals.
  • Four role-scoped CRM dashboards (Paramedic, Doctor, Admin, Audit) subscribe to the same SSE stream, so every agent action shows up live in the right view for the right person.
  • EHR writes only execute under a verified physician token after approval in the Doctor CRM; field drafts run under the paramedic's token, and a SHA-256 audit chain links every step in between.
  • Next.js monorepo, LangChain chains for the agents, Supabase/Postgres for persistence, and a clinical eval suite (anaphylaxis, STEMI, stroke-on-Warfarin) gating changes in CI.

Recourse

View source↗

When an institution denies a claim, the rules it broke are usually its own. Recourse reads the denial letter, retrieves the institution's published policy, and proves where the denial contradicts it, then drafts a cited appeal, waits for a human yes, files it, and tracks the deadline.

  • Recourse reads an institutional denial letter, pulls the institution's own published policy, and proves where the denial contradicts it. The contradiction engine is deterministic: it parses the policy into a criteria checklist and checks each one. Five of five caught in evals, zero false appeals.
  • Thirteen agents run in two phases under a custom orchestrator with typed retry logic; intake handles scanned letters through OCR.
  • Deadline and eligibility math runs as real code in a sandboxed runtime, never inside the LLM; the model only ever sees the results.
  • Once a human approves the drafted appeal, filing falls back from portal to fax automatically, and strategy memory carries what worked into the next case.

SignSpeak

View source↗

A low-cost device for two-way conversation between Deaf and hearing users: it translates sign language into speech, and spoken replies into on-screen text, fully offline, on hardware that costs less than a textbook. Took 2nd at ACSEF 2025.

  • Took 2nd place at ACSEF 2025. A wearable that translates ASL into speech, and spoken replies into on-screen text, fully offline on a Raspberry Pi 4 with sub-2-second inference.
  • MediaPipe extracts hand landmarks frame by frame through OpenCV; a fine-tuned MobileNetV2 covers 36 static signs at 95% validation accuracy, and a TCN plus bidirectional LSTM cover dynamic gestures at 90%.
  • Data was the bottleneck: fewer than 500 raw clips became 14,000+ training samples through an augmentation pipeline built on the landmarks.
  • Models export to TorchScript and ONNX for the Pi, paired with Vosk speech-to-text running locally for the reply direction.
